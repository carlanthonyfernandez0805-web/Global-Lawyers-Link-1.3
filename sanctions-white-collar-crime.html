Sanctions Exposure

How Sanctions Exposure Arises

Sanctions risk is often broader, and less obvious, than it first appears.

01

Indirect Dealings

Transacting with a sanctioned individual, entity, or country indirectly, through intermediaries, can still create exposure.

02

Divergent Sanctions Lists

Conduct lawful under one country’s sanctions regime may still violate another’s list.

03

Individual Officer Exposure

A corporate sanctions violation can expose individual directors and officers personally, not just the company.

04

Extraterritorial Reach

Some sanctions and anti-bribery regimes apply to conduct with only a limited connection to the enforcing country.

White Collar Crime Considerations

Frequently Asked

Common Questions

Can I violate sanctions without dealing directly with a sanctioned country?

Yes — exposure can arise through intermediaries or indirect transactions, even without a direct dealing with the sanctioned party.

Can a company’s self-disclosure affect my individual exposure?

Yes, a company’s cooperation or disclosure decisions can significantly shape how individual employees or officers are subsequently treated.

Do anti-bribery laws apply to conduct outside the enforcing country?

Increasingly yes — many statutes now apply extraterritorially where only a limited connection to the enforcing country exists.

Can I face civil, regulatory, and criminal proceedings for the same conduct?

Yes, these can run in parallel, and a response to one can directly affect the others.
